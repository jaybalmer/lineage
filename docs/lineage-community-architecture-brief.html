Architecture Evolution

Community
as Lens

Lineage moves from a single-sport prototype to a multi-community platform. Communities are scoped lenses over a shared graph, not isolated silos. Nodes join communities. Claims belong to one.

Pre-Implementation
01

Context and Intent

The current prototype is built entirely within the snowboarding context. While snowboarding remains the launch community, the underlying architecture needs to support many communities from day one so that the prototype can scale without structural rework.

This brief defines how communities are modeled, how nodes relate to communities, how claims are scoped, and how the personal timeline adapts across community contexts.

Guiding Principle

Communities are lenses over the graph, not partitions of it. The same rider, place, brand, or event can appear in multiple communities. What stays community-specific is the claim: the story, gear entry, or event participation that a person creates within a community context.

02

URL Structure and Routing

Each community occupies a top-level route under the Lineage domain. All pages within that community live under its path prefix.

lineage.wtf/— landing page, community discovery
lineage.wtf/snowboarding— community home
lineage.wtf/snowboarding/riders/brad-holmes— rider profile, scoped
lineage.wtf/snowboarding/places/whistler— place page, scoped
lineage.wtf/snowboarding/brands/burton— brand page, scoped
lineage.wtf/snowboarding/events/arctic-challenge-2003— event page, scoped

The landing page at lineage.wtf serves as the community directory. Snowboarding is the only live community at launch. Additional communities are visible but marked as forthcoming.

03

Node Membership Model

The four primary node types in the Lineage graph each carry a community membership list. Membership determines where a node surfaces, not where it lives. The underlying record is shared across the graph.

Node Type
People
A rider joins communities. Brad can be a member of snowboarding, surf, and skate simultaneously. His profile node is one record.
Node Type
Places
Whistler belongs to snowboarding, skiing, and mountain biking. The same Place node surfaces in all three community maps.
Node Type
Brands
A brand like Burton or Oakley can hold membership in multiple communities and appears in each community's brand directory.
Node Type
Events
A mountain festival may span MTB and snowboard communities. Events carry a community list and surface in all relevant calendars.

04

Claim Scoping

Claims are the core contribution type on Lineage: a story, gear entry, event participation, or sourced reference created by a user or publisher. Unlike nodes, claims are not shared across communities.

Every claim is created within a community context and stays there. If Brad wants to document the same trip across two communities, those are two separate claims. There is no automatic propagation.

Claim Scoping Rules

Scenario Behavior
Brad adds a snowboard gear entry Snowboarding only Does not appear on his surf or skate timeline
Brad adds a surf session story Surf only Does not appear on his snowboard timeline
Brad attends a multi-sport festival with communities snowboard + MTB The Event node surfaces in both communities. Brad's participation claim is created in one community unless he explicitly claims it in both separately.
A journalist publishes a story that references Whistler The citation is attached to whichever community context the story was published under. Whistler the Place node surfaces in all relevant communities, but the journalistic citation belongs to one.
A brand sponsors an event straddling two communities The brand node appears in both communities. The sponsorship relationship is authored within a specific community context unless explicitly tagged to both.
Design Decision

Cross-community claim linking is reserved as a future capability, not a V1 feature. In V1, claims belong to exactly one community. This keeps claim provenance clean and avoids ambiguity in the personal timeline.

05

Personal Timeline Behavior

The personal timeline adapts based on where the user is viewing it. Context determines scope.

Home Context (lineage.wtf)

The merged timeline shows all first-person claims across every community the user has joined. A community filter allows narrowing to a single community without leaving the home context. This is the only place cross-community claims appear side by side.

Community Context (lineage.wtf/snowboarding)

The timeline shows only claims created within the snowboarding community. No surf or skate claims surface here, even if the viewer is Brad and he is a member of all three.

Tab Structure on Profile

Profile tabs are generated dynamically from the user's community memberships. The "All" tab is always present and always first. Community tabs appear in join order or by activity volume (to be decided).

06

Landing Page and Community Discovery

The Lineage landing page at lineage.wtf serves two purposes: it is the home for authenticated users (merged timeline) and the discovery entry point for new visitors.

For Authenticated Users

The default view is the merged personal timeline. A community switcher or tab row gives quick access to each joined community. The landing page does not redirect to a specific community route; it stays at root.

For New Visitors

The page presents available communities. At launch, only snowboarding is live. Other communities (surf, skate, ski, MTB) are displayed as forthcoming to signal the platform's scope without overpromising access.

07

Cross-Community Overlaps

Shared nodes are the connective tissue of the graph. When a place, brand, or person appears in multiple communities, that overlap is a feature, not a conflict. The question is how explicitly to surface it in the UI.

Surfacing Overlap (Recommended)

Node pages within a community context should display a secondary indicator showing other communities that node belongs to. This serves as natural cross-discovery without routing the user away.

Example: The Whistler page at lineage.wtf/snowboarding/places/whistler displays a note that this place also belongs to the skiing and MTB communities, with links to those community-scoped views of the same place.

Overlap Does Not Mean Shared Claims

The overlap indicator points to the same underlying Place node viewed through a different community lens. The claims attached to Whistler in the skiing community are not visible on the snowboarding community page and vice versa, unless a claim was explicitly tagged to multiple communities (a future capability, not V1).

08

Implementation Decisions

The following decisions were resolved prior to implementation handoff.

Decision Resolution
Claim community assignment Route inference. The claim automatically inherits the community from the route the user is on when creating it. No explicit picker in V1.
Cross-community claim duplication Not supported in V1. A user creates a fresh claim per community independently. Duplication mechanic deferred to a future version.
Profile tab ordering Sorted by claim activity volume, descending. The community with the most claims surfaces first. Not join date order.
Node community membership gating Restricted to users with Editor flag set to TRUE or Founding member status. Community admin roles are deferred. At launch this is a single-operator function.
Onboarding community selection Community selection is an explicit step at the start of onboarding. Snowboarding is the only selectable option at launch. All subsequent onboarding steps are driven by the selected community. The snowboard flow (first day, boards, places, events) is the template for future community onboarding builds.
Community membership on public profile Community memberships are displayed as tags or badges above the timeline on a rider's profile. Selecting a community filters the timeline to that community's claims. A second tier of filters then becomes available for categories within that community (e.g. gear, events, places).
